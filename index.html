Research

The base of the continental crust is typically marked by a strong seismic discontinuity due to a sharp contrast in seismic velocities and densities between crust and underlying mantle. Moho depth and lower crustal character is a reflection of the tectonic amalgamation and magmatic evolution of the continents. We examine Moho topography and lower crustal character in the western U.S. using receiver functions (RFs) and common conversion point stacking (CCP). Data derive from the Earthscope Transportable Array and other suitable seismic stations, with 2198 stations in total. P waves from 3764 suitable earthquakes recorded at these stations were downloaded from the IRIS DMC. We estimate Moho depths directly from the CCP stacks.

Our CCP stacking result with additional information. (B)USGS tectonic province boundary. (C) Precambrian province boundary. (D) Approximate 7.x layer locations.

These results compare well with extant models of Moho depth in the western U.S. (Crust 1.0; Buehler and Shearer (2017)).

P-to-S transmission coefficient occurs at Moho

In addition to Moho depths, we calculate lower crustal densities from estimates of the P-to-S conversion transmission coefficients (TCs). We use crustal seismic velocities from Shen and Ritzwoller (2016) and uppermost mantle seismic velocities from Buehler and Shearer (2017) to determine TCs of P-to-S conversions at the Moho for three crustal models, Crust 1.0, Buehler and Shearer, and our CCP stacking Moho model. We used both constant (3.35 g/cm3) and regionally varying uppermost mantle density estimates (Chulick & Mooney (2002)). We also estimated TCs from the RF amplitudes. Lower crustal densities are most sensitive to seismic velocities - both P and S - in the lowermost crust.

In order to estimate uncertainties in estimated lower crustal densities, we perturbed values of the seismic velocity models using a random number generator with Gaussian distribution, thereby introducing 'noise' in the resulting RFs. TCs and densities where recalculated, yielding mean values and standard deviations of the lower crustal densities. We find thinner crustal thicknesses alog the Rocky Mountain Front(RMF) than those reported in Crust 1.0 or Buehler & Shearer. Apparently thin crust from RFs also correlates with loci of double-peaked anisotropic RFs, as reported by Schulte-Pelkum et al. (2017). Thus, apparently thin crust along the RMF is likely due to presence of a lower crustal 7.X layer; seismic velocity contrast at the top of this layer generates the strongest mode conversion in the CCP stacks.
